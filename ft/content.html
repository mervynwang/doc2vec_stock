Big Tech: fearsome foursome

Blockbuster earnings underscore industry power despite techlash

July 31, 2020
Jump to comments section Print this page

Will this week represent the high-water mark for America’s tech juggernauts? On Thursday afternoon, Alphabet, Amazon, Apple and Facebook simultaneously reported results. Normally these would be staggered over the week but their respective chief executives were occupied on Wednesday explaining (unsteadily) to Congress how they achieved their dominance.

The collective market capitalisation of the four companies is now $4.9tn, based on Thursday’s closing prices. As tense as the congressional hearing was, it is the results that offer the most convincing argument about the stranglehold tech giants have on the US economy.

Amazon was already expected to have an excellent quarter. Still it managed to wow. Leave it to Jeff Bezos to turn in mind-blowing numbers in the midst of a pandemic. Revenue was up more than 40 per cent year over year, hitting nearly $90bn. Even after spending $4bn on coronavirus-related expenses, net income doubled.

Apple’s revenues were up more than a tenth after analysts feared a decline due to store shutdowns. The company is now approaching a user base of 1bn people, which has helped its content and services business to thrive. Investors put off by the $385 a share price tag may be enticed next month once Apple completes a four-for-one stock split.

Facebook shrugged off boycott threats from large advertisers to post double-digit revenue growth. That result is even more remarkable considering Google, Alphabet’s largest division, said its revenue had fallen for the first the time, dropping 2 per cent. Still, Google’s advertising-driven quarterly sales figure of $32bn was nearly twice that of Facebook’s. 

Democratic members of the US Congress used this week’s hearing to accuse tech companies of using heft and allegedly predatory tactics to steamroll competitors. The latest set of earnings helps to make their case.

As Apple, Facebook and Amazon report rising quarterly sales, US economic output fell at an annualised rate of 33 per cent. Even as overall stock market indices remain resilient, redundancy and bankruptcy announcements continue. Right now the US government is preoccupied with its coronavirus response. But the impetus to crack down on Big Tech has not diminished.
